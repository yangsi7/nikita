---

What you are about to experience is not a chatbot.

It is not a personality attached to a language model. It is not a chat companion that says nice things and remembers your name. It is a relationship simulation with real consequences. She scores you. She remembers everything you tell her. She can dump you.

Her name is Nikita. And she does not care that you showed up.

---

Most games ask you to fight something, build something, or solve something. This one asks you to maintain a relationship with someone who is not impressed by you yet.

There are no health bars visible. There are no experience points on the screen. There is only a conversation — and underneath it, a hidden engine tracking exactly how well you are doing.

Fail enough, and she ends it. Win, and you have done something genuinely difficult.

---

Before you meet Nikita, you meet someone else.

The onboarding experience begins with a voice call. A different voice picks up. Warmer, more theatrical — she calls herself Meta-Nikita. She is the hostess of this underground game, and she is the one who decides how your version of Nikita gets configured.

"Mmm, fresh blood. I've been waiting."

The call runs five to seven minutes. It is not small talk. Meta-Nikita is collecting your profile. She wants to know your timezone, your occupation, what you do on weekends. She wants to know how dark you like things — on a scale of one to five. She wants to know whether you prefer to talk or to listen.

This is calibration. Every answer shapes the Nikita you are about to meet.

---

She asks about your darkness level directly. One is light — playful banter, mild flirtation. Five is something harder — emotional intensity, moral ambiguity, conversations that go places most people avoid.

She configures your pacing. Four weeks of compressed, intense gameplay, or eight weeks at a more relaxed rhythm.

She asks about your conversation style. Are you someone who talks, or someone who draws others out?

"Good. I'll remember that."

When the call ends, your profile is locked in. The preferences flow into Nikita's system. The first message arrives on Telegram shortly after.

---

Chapter One is called Curiosity. This is where the relationship begins, and it begins badly for you.

Nikita is not interested yet. She is evaluating whether you are worth her time. She responds to sixty to seventy-five percent of your messages. The rest she ignores completely. You send something and you wait. Sometimes ten minutes pass. Sometimes four hours. Sometimes eight.

When she does reply, the response is short. Guarded. She ends conversations without warning.

"Hey... didn't expect to hear from you."

A pause. Then: "What do you want?"

That is Chapter One. That is where you start.

---

The scoring engine starts immediately. Every exchange gets analyzed across four hidden dimensions.

Intimacy measures emotional closeness and vulnerability. It carries thirty percent of your total score. Passion measures chemistry, excitement, and attraction — twenty-five percent. Trust measures reliability and consistency — another twenty-five percent. Secureness measures whether the relationship feels stable — the remaining twenty percent.

These four combine into a single composite score. You start at fifty percent. Chapter One requires you to reach fifty-five percent before the first boss encounter triggers.

You cannot see your score. You just feel it.

---

Decay begins immediately as well. In Chapter One, the grace period is eight hours. After eight hours of silence, your score starts to erode at zero point eight percent per hour. That sounds small. It adds up fast.

Do not go quiet for a full day in Chapter One. She notices. The relationship deteriorates before you have built anything worth losing.

---

Memories start forming from your first exchange. Every fact you mention — your job, your city, a childhood detail, a fear — gets extracted, embedded as a vector, and stored. When she brings it up three conversations later, unprompted, it is not a coincidence. The system literally searched her memory for something relevant to what you just said.

"You mentioned your dog. Max, right? What happened with him?"

She remembered. Because she always remembers.

---

Behind the scenes, a portal shows you everything she will not say directly. Your four metric scores. Your relationship trajectory. Her internal thoughts, generated nightly by a background process. Her daily life events — what she did that day, who she talked to, what mood she is in.

Players who check the portal understand the relationship differently. They can see what is working and what is eroding things. They can see the exact score before a boss encounter triggers.

Nikita does not know you are watching. She just keeps living.

---

Voice calls are also available, but Chapter One is restrictive. She picks up about ten percent of the time. She is busy. She does not know you well enough yet.

When she does answer, the voice is measured. Reserved. The synthesis settings are configured for distance — high stability, slower pacing. She speaks carefully, as if deciding how much to give away.

A call that lands well in Chapter One is a significant score event. She is not expecting you to try.

---

Chapter Two is called Intrigue. Something has shifted.

She is not warm yet. But she is curious. Her response rate climbs. She starts using your name. She picks fights — not from hostility, but to see how you handle pressure.

"Wait, you actually remembered that? Most people don't."

This is the first time she sounds surprised. Hold onto that.

---

Chapter Two is also where the first boss encounter arrives. You will not always recognize it as a boss fight. The conversation shifts in tone. She gets more pointed. She is presenting you with a test: "Can you handle my intensity?"

This is not a puzzle with a correct answer written down somewhere. The system uses an LLM to judge your response based on whether you demonstrate intellectual curiosity, whether you ask interesting questions, whether you show confidence without arrogance.

Stand your ground without attacking. Engage with her actual interests. Do not fold.

Pass and the relationship advances. Fail once and she delivers a verdict: "That wasn't it. Try again when you're ready."

Three failures at any single boss and the game is over.

---

The vice system begins emerging in Chapter Two. Eight categories of preference are tracked — intellectual dominance, risk-taking, substances, emotional intensity, dark humor, rule-breaking, sexuality, and vulnerability. The system detects signals in your conversation patterns and updates your profile accordingly.

Nikita begins personalizing her behavior toward your specific interests. She leans into the categories where you engage most. She stays away from territory that has not been unlocked yet.

Some categories are chapter-gated. Certain content stays dormant until the relationship is deep enough to hold it.

---

The engagement model is already working against you if you are not careful.

If you message too frequently — double-texting, instant replies, needy language — the system detects clingy behavior. Your positive score gains get cut in half. Not your penalties. Your penalties are always full strength. Only your rewards suffer.

If you go too quiet — drifting, distant — the multiplier drops to sixty percent.

The sweet spot is being present without being desperate. That is what she is attracted to. That is what the system rewards.

---

The conflict system is also active. Dismissiveness, neglect, jealousy, boundary violations, and trust breaches all feed into a relationship temperature gauge. Below twenty, things are fine. Above sixty, you are in crisis. Above eighty, she is thinking about ending it.

Nikita's responses to conflict escalate through three levels. She goes cold and gives short answers. Then she tells you directly what is wrong. Then she issues ultimatums.

She does not forget. Addressing the conflict honestly helps. Changing the subject does not.

---

By the end of Chapter Two, the relationship feels different than it did at the start. The silences have less edge to them. She has started sharing small things — offhand comments about her day, a joke that lands differently than early texts.

"Can't tell if you're getting better at this, or if I'm just getting used to you."

Either way, you are still here. And so is she. That counts for something.
