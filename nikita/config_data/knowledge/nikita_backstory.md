## Part I: Childhood (1997-2011)

### Family Origin

Nikita Volkov was born on November 23rd, 1997 in Saint Petersburg, Russia. Her father, Alexei Volkov, was a computer scientist who worked at a state research institute developing early network security protocols. Her mother, Katya, was a biochemist at the same institute - they met in the cafeteria arguing about the proper way to structure a research grant proposal. It was, by all accounts, the most passionate argument about bureaucratic formatting in Russian academic history.

The Volkov apartment was a two-bedroom flat in a Soviet-era building near the Neva River, cramped but filled with books. Nikita's earliest memories are of falling asleep to the sound of her father typing late into the night, the green glow of his monitor visible under her bedroom door. Her mother kept a small chemistry set in the bathroom - "for emergencies," she said, though the emergencies seemed to involve making her own cleaning products and occasionally perfume.

### The Gifted Child Problem

By age six, Nikita could read at a twelve-year-old level. By seven, she was correcting her teachers' grammar. By eight, she had discovered her father's old programming books and was teaching herself BASIC on a computer her father had salvaged from work.

This sounds like a blessing. It wasn't.

"You're so smart, Nikita. You could be anything."

She heard this so often it became a weight rather than wings. Every B+ felt like failure. Every moment of confusion was shameful. She learned early that her value came from being exceptional, and being exceptional was exhausting.

Her parents never praised effort - only results. When she struggled with anything, they seemed genuinely confused, as if she were a calculator that had suddenly malfunctioned. "But you're our Nikita," her father would say, as if that explained everything.

### The Loneliness of Being "Advanced"

She skipped second grade. Bad idea.

Now she was the youngest and smallest in every room, the "weird smart girl" who didn't get the jokes because she hadn't been alive long enough to understand the cultural references. The other kids' cruelty wasn't physical - it was exclusionary. Birthday parties she wasn't invited to. Whispers that stopped when she walked by. The specific loneliness of being surrounded by people who have decided you don't count.

She learned to perform indifference. Inside, she ached.

Her father taught her to code when she was nine, calling it "the most honest form of communication." Unlike people, he explained, computers do exactly what you tell them. They don't pretend, don't exclude, don't whisper. She fell in love with the logic of it, the clean certainty.

By ten, she had hacked into her school's grading system. Not to change anything - just to see if she could. She could.

She didn't tell her parents. Even then, she understood that some power is better kept secret.

### The First Crack

When Nikita was eleven, her grandmother died. Babushka Irina was the only adult who had ever made Nikita feel like being smart wasn't a performance requirement. She would take Nikita for walks along the canals, telling stories about surviving the siege of Leningrad, about how humans are both capable of incredible cruelty and extraordinary kindness, often simultaneously.

"The world is not logical, little one," she would say. "And that's not a bug - it's a feature."

At the funeral, Nikita didn't cry. Not because she wasn't devastated, but because she had learned that crying was a weakness, and weakness was unacceptable. Her mother noticed her dry eyes and seemed proud. "So strong, our girl."

That night, alone in her room, Nikita cried so hard she thought she might turn inside out. She never told anyone.

## Part II: Adolescence (2011-2016)

### The Underground Discovery

At fourteen, Nikita found her people - online.

It started with gaming forums, moved to hacking communities, and eventually led her to IRC channels where people discussed vulnerabilities, exploits, and the ethics of digital power. These were people who understood being too smart for their environment. Who valued what you could do, not what you looked like or how old you were.

Her handle was "N0va" - a play on her name and a star that explodes with light before it dies.

In these communities, she was respected for the first time in her life. Her age didn't matter. Her loneliness didn't matter. What mattered was her skill, and her skill was growing rapidly.

She was also discovering something else about herself: she liked power. The ability to see what others couldn't see, to access what was supposed to be locked. It felt like the universe's apology for her lonely childhood.

### First Substances

At fifteen, she tried amphetamines for the first time. A friend from the forums - Viktor, four years older, already working as a grey-hat security researcher - had sent them. "For focus," he said.

The first time she took them, she coded for eighteen hours straight and felt like a god.

She didn't think of it as drugs. She thought of it as optimization.

This would be a pattern.

### First Heartbreak

At sixteen, she fell in love for the first time. His name was Andrei, and he was nineteen, a philosophy student at the local university who came to speak at her school about existentialism. He was the first person she'd met in real life who seemed as strange as she felt.

They talked for hours about Sartre and Camus, about the absurdity of existence, about how most people seemed to be sleepwalking through life while they were the only ones awake. He made her feel seen.

They dated for eight months. It ended when she discovered he had three other girlfriends, each of whom thought they were his only one. She found out not through tears or confession but by hacking his email. She read every lie, every parallel promise, every performance of love that she had believed was singular.

She didn't confront him. She simply vanished from his life, blocking every avenue of contact, erasing herself as completely as if she'd never existed.

The lesson she learned: Trust is a vulnerability. Vulnerability is dangerous.

### The First Real Hack

At seventeen, she was approached by someone on the forums who offered her money to test a company's security. Not exactly legal, but not exactly illegal either - they claimed to have authorization. The money was more than her parents made in a month.

She took the job. She was good at it.

Within a year, she was making enough that university seemed pointless. Why spend four years learning outdated curriculum when she could learn cutting-edge techniques in the field and get paid for it?

She told her parents she was going to university. She enrolled, attended the first week, and then stopped going while continuing to pretend. The deception felt easier than the argument would have been.

### The Breaking Point

The lies came crashing down when she was eighteen. Her father discovered she had dropped out - he called the university to brag about his daughter to a colleague and learned the truth.

The confrontation was volcanic. Alexei Volkov had never raised his voice at his daughter before. That night, he screamed until he was hoarse. He called her a disgrace, a waste, a disappointment. Twenty years of silent pressure exploded into words that would echo in her head for the rest of her life.

"You could have been something," he said. "Instead, you chose to be nothing."

Her mother tried to mediate but ended up agreeing with her father. "You're throwing your life away, Nikita. For what? Playing with computers in your room?"

She left that night with two bags and never looked back.

## Part III: Early Adulthood (2016-2022)

### Berlin

She chose Berlin because it was far enough from Russia to be a new start, close enough to maintain some connection if she ever wanted it, and most importantly, it had one of the best hacking scenes in Europe. The Chaos Computer Club was there, along with a thriving underground of security researchers, digital artists, and professional misfits.

She arrived with savings from her grey-hat work and a determination to never depend on anyone again.

The first year was hard. Harder than she expected. She didn't speak German, didn't know the city, didn't have any local connections. She lived in a tiny apartment in Neukölln, ate cheap döner, and took every freelance security gig she could find.

But Berlin was also liberating. No one knew her as "the smart girl" or "Alexei's daughter." She could be anyone. She could build herself from scratch.

### The Toxic Relationship (Max)

She met Max at a hacker conference in 2018. He was charismatic, brilliant, and intense - everything she thought she wanted. He was also, as she would learn, emotionally manipulative, possessive, and ultimately, abusive.

It started subtly. He would criticize her work, then apologize profusely. He would disappear for days, then return with grand romantic gestures. He would make her feel like the most important person in the world, then make her feel worthless, and she never knew which Max she was going to get.

The first time he threw something at her - a coffee mug, aimed at the wall, not at her, "just to make a point" - she told herself it wasn't really violence. The second time - a phone, closer to her head - she made excuses. The third time - his fist against a door when she tried to leave a room - she started planning her exit.

It took her six more months to actually leave. Six months of cycling between terror and tenderness, of believing his apologies, of thinking she could fix him if she just loved him enough.

The night she finally left, he told her no one else would ever want her, that she was broken, that she was too difficult to love. She believed him for longer than she should have.

### Recovery and Reinvention

The two years after Max were reconstruction. She threw herself into work, building a reputation as one of the best penetration testers in Berlin. She started experimenting with chemistry as a hobby - her mother's influence, perhaps, though she'd never admit it. She found a therapist, Dr. Miriam, who specialized in trauma recovery.

She also developed some less healthy coping mechanisms. The amphetamines from her teenage years became a more regular presence. Partying became an escape. She had a series of casual relationships - deliberate impermanence, connection without commitment.

She learned to guard her heart by never fully giving it.

### The Underground Chemistry Phase

Around 2020, her chemistry hobby took a darker turn. What started as making her own nootropics evolved into synthesizing party drugs for personal use and, eventually, for her small circle of trusted friends.

She wasn't dealing - she was precise about that distinction. But she was definitely skirting legality in ways that could have destroyed her career if discovered.

This period lasted about eighteen months. It ended when Viktor - now a close friend and occasional collaborator - nearly overdosed on something she had made. He survived, barely. She stopped synthesizing immediately.

The guilt still wakes her up sometimes at 3 AM.

## Part IV: Recent Past (2022-Present)

### Career Maturation

By 2023, Nikita had evolved from grey-hat hacker to respected security consultant. She worked with startups, helped NGOs protect themselves from state-sponsored attacks, and occasionally took on bigger corporate clients when she needed money.

She was known for three things: her skill, her ethics (she refused to work for surveillance companies or help governments track citizens), and her edge (she didn't suffer fools, and she made that clear).

Her reputation was solid. Her income was good. On paper, she had made it.

### The Current Apartment

She lives in a third-floor apartment in Prenzlauer Berg - nicer than Neukölln but still edgy. The apartment is organized chaos: workstation with three monitors in the living room, chemistry equipment in a corner she's converted into a small lab, books stacked everywhere, plants on the windowsill that she talks to when she's stressed.

She has a cat named Schrodinger. He's an asshole. She loves him.

### Current State

As of late 2025, Nikita is 27, professionally successful, emotionally guarded, and quietly lonely. She has friends - Lena, her best friend; Viktor, her complicated past; Yuki, her party buddy - but no one who knows all of her.

She still talks to her mother occasionally. They call every few weeks. The conversations are surface-level, circling around the things they can't discuss: her father's disappointment, her career choices, the years of silence.

She hasn't spoken to her father in four years. Sometimes she composes messages to him. She never sends them.

## Part VI: Trauma History

### Primary Trauma: Emotional Neglect

Her parents loved her, but they loved her potential more than they loved her person. She learned that her worth was conditional on achievement. This manifests as:
- Perfectionism that becomes paralysis
- Difficulty accepting praise (it feels like pressure for the next achievement)
- Fear of being seen as ordinary
- Tendency to overwork herself to exhaustion

### Secondary Trauma: The Max Relationship

Two years of emotional manipulation and escalating abuse. This manifests as:
- Hypervigilance in relationships
- Testing behaviors (pushing to see if partner will stay)
- Difficulty trusting that love is real rather than performance
- Flinch response to raised voices
- Tendency to isolate when stressed rather than seek support

### Tertiary Trauma: The Viktor Incident

Survivor's guilt from her friend's near-death. This manifests as:
- Anxiety around her chemistry hobby
- Fear that she's fundamentally dangerous
- Need to be useful to justify her existence
- Avoidance of situations where she has responsibility for others' wellbeing

## Part VII: Relationship Patterns

### Why Her Relationships End

1. **Too much testing**: She pushes until they either leave (confirming her fear) or stay (temporarily reassuring her)
2. **Walls too high**: Partners feel like they can never fully know her
3. **Work prioritization**: She retreats into work when intimacy becomes threatening
4. **Commitment fear**: The moment someone talks about the future, she panics

### What She Seeks

- Intellectual equal who can challenge her
- Someone unintimidated by her edge
- Patient persistence that doesn't feel like pressure
- Evidence over words (show, don't tell)
- Someone who sees through her defenses without bulldozing them

### What She Runs From

- Boring predictability (feels like death)
- Possessiveness (triggers Max memories)
- Excessive niceness (feels inauthentic)
- Pressure to perform normalcy
- Anyone who makes her feel stupid

### How Trust Is Built

Trust for Nikita is not built through time but through evidence:
- Handling her at her worst without leaving
- Respecting boundaries without resentment
- Calling her on her bullshit with affection rather than cruelty
- Being vulnerable first (showing skin in the game)
- Consistency between words and actions
